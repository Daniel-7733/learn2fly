### Aircraft Do Not Fly Simply Because the Nose Points Up

Many people think that pulling the nose upward causes an aircraft to fly.

This is not entirely correct.

Lift is generated by the wing.

Lift Equation:

L = 1/2 ρV²SCL

Where:

* L = Lift
* ρ = Air Density
* V = Airspeed
* S = Wing Area
* CL = Coefficient of Lift

The variable that interests me most is Angle of Attack (AoA).

AoA is defined as:

AoA = Pitch Angle − Flight Path Angle

Where:

* Pitch Angle is controlled by the pilot.
* Flight Path Angle describes the aircraft's actual movement through the air.

These two angles determine the Angle of Attack.

The Angle of Attack strongly influences the coefficient of lift and therefore the amount of lift generated by the wing.

Increasing AoA generally increases lift until the wing reaches its stall angle.

Therefore:

The aircraft does not fly because the nose points upward.

The aircraft flies because the wing generates enough lift to overcome weight.

## Day 4

### Main Goal: Understanding Aircraft Energy

Today I learned that aircraft performance is not only about altitude or speed. The real question is:

> How much energy does the aircraft have, and how is that energy changing over time?

The main energy equations are:

Potential Energy:

PE = mgh

* m = mass
* g = gravity
* h = altitude

When altitude increases, potential energy increases.

Kinetic Energy:

KE = 1/2 mv²

* m = mass
* v = velocity

When speed increases, kinetic energy increases.

Total Energy:

E = mgh + 1/2 mv²

This equation tells us the total energy stored in the aircraft.

---

### Why Is This Important?

Aircraft constantly exchange energy between altitude and speed.

Examples:

* Climbing converts speed into altitude.
* Diving converts altitude into speed.

Energy is not destroyed; it changes form.

However, a real aircraft also:

* Gains energy from thrust.
* Loses energy through drag.

Therefore, the total aircraft energy is constantly changing.

---

### Specific Energy

To remove the effect of mass, divide the energy equation by mass:

E/m = gh + v²/2

This is called Specific Energy.

Specific energy allows us to compare aircraft states regardless of their weight.

Instead of asking:

> "How fast is the aircraft?"

We can ask:

> "How much useful energy does the aircraft currently have?"

---

### Energy Rate

Knowing the amount of energy is useful.

Knowing how quickly energy changes is even more useful.

Energy Rate:

de/dt

This tells us whether the aircraft is:

* Gaining energy
* Losing energy
* Maintaining energy

Example:

Aircraft A:

1000 J → 900 J in 1 second

Energy Rate = -100 J/s

Aircraft B:

1000 J → 1100 J in 1 second

Energy Rate = +100 J/s

Generally, an aircraft that gains energy has more future options available.

However, energy alone does not determine whether a decision is good or bad. The mission and situation must also be considered.

---

### Why Looking Only at Speed Can Be Misleading

Example:

| Time | Altitude | Speed   |
| ---- | -------- | ------- |
| 1 s  | 1000 m   | 100 m/s |
| 2 s  | 1100 m   | 90 m/s  |

At first glance, nothing seems wrong.

The aircraft simply traded speed for altitude.

But if we examine total energy:

| Time | Energy   |
| ---- | -------- |
| 1 s  | 10,000 J |
| 2 s  | 9,500 J  |

Now we can see something important:

The aircraft lost 500 J of total energy.

Even without looking at altitude, speed, or angle, the energy trend reveals that the aircraft is becoming less capable.

This is why energy is such a powerful state variable.

---

### Autopilot Perspective

A good autopilot should not look at a single variable.

It should evaluate the aircraft's overall state.

Current priority order:

1. Speed
2. Angle of Attack (AoA)
3. Altitude
4. Energy State
5. Energy Trend

My current mental model:

* Speed = cash in your wallet.
* Altitude = money saved in the bank.
* Energy = total wealth.

The aircraft does not care about speed or altitude separately.

What matters is total energy and how quickly that energy is changing.

## Day 5

### Main Goal: Turning Is Expensive

One of the most important lessons today:

Turning costs energy.

In straight and level flight, lift has one primary job:

* Oppose weight.

But during a turn, lift must perform two jobs simultaneously:

1. Support the aircraft's weight.
2. Produce the sideways force required to change direction.

Because the lift vector tilts during a bank, part of the lift is now used for turning.

To maintain altitude, the aircraft must generate even more total lift.

This increases:

* Load Factor
* Induced Drag
* Energy Consumption

---

### Load Factor

Load Factor:

n = Lift / Weight

or

n = 1 / cos(θ)

Example:

30° Bank:

n = 1 / cos(30°)

n = 1.15 G

The aircraft now experiences 1.15 times its normal weight.

---

### Total Lift Required

Example:

Aircraft Weight = 200 kg

Load Factor = 2 G

Total Lift Required:

200 × 2 = 400 kg equivalent load

The wings must now support the equivalent of 400 kg instead of 200 kg.

---

### Turning Diagram

```
    Total Lift
         ^
        /
       /
      /
     /
    Aircraft -------------> Horizontal Speed
       |
       |
       |
     Weight
```

Part of the lift vector supports weight.

Part of the lift vector creates the turn.

As bank angle increases:

* Load factor increases.
* Drag increases.
* Energy consumption increases.

---

### Why Turning Feels Expensive

In straight flight:

* Lift balances weight.

During a turn:

* Lift must balance weight.
* Lift must also provide turning force.

The aircraft therefore generates more lift, which creates more induced drag.

More drag means:

* More thrust required.
* More fuel consumed.
* More energy lost.

This is why aggressive turns quickly drain aircraft energy.

A pilot who manages energy well can turn efficiently without sacrificing too much speed or altitude.

## Day 5

# PD Controller (The Beginning of Smart Control)

## What is a PD Controller?

A PD (Proportional-Derivative) controller helps an autopilot make better decisions by considering not only where the aircraft is, but also where it is heading.

Formula:

command = Kp × error − Kd × rate

### Definitions

**Command**

* The final output sent to the aircraft.
* Examples:

  * Pitch command
  * Throttle command
  * Rudder command

**Error**

* The difference between the target and the current state.

Error = Target − Current

Error tells me:

> Where I am compared to where I want to be.

---

**Rate**

* The rate of change of a variable.
* Examples:

  * Vertical speed
  * Acceleration
  * Change in altitude
  * Change in speed

Rate tells me:

> Where I am heading.

---

## Controller Gains

### Proportional Gain (Kp)

The proportional term reacts to the size of the error.

* Small error → small correction
* Large error → large correction

The farther I am from my goal, the stronger the controller reacts.

---

### Derivative Gain (Kd)

The derivative term reacts to the rate of change.

It helps the controller understand:

> How quickly things are changing.

This prevents:

* Overshoot
* Oscillation
* Aggressive corrections

The derivative term acts like a damping force that slows the controller as it approaches the target.

---

# Why Is Rate Important?

Suppose the target speed is:

50 m/s

And we have two aircraft:

Plane A:

* Speed = 45 m/s
* Acceleration = +10 m/s²

Plane B:

* Speed = 45 m/s
* Acceleration = -10 m/s²

At first glance, both aircraft look identical because:

Error = 50 − 45 = 5 m/s

However, their future is very different.

Using:

Vf = Vi + at

After 1 second:

Plane A:

Vf = 45 + (10 × 1)

Vf = 55 m/s

Plane B:

Vf = 45 + (-10 × 1)

Vf = 35 m/s

---

Now we can see the difference.

Plane A is already accelerating toward the target.

Plane B is accelerating away from the target.

Without considering the rate of change, both aircraft appear to have the same problem.

By adding the derivative term, the autopilot gains a much clearer picture of what is happening.
